# Financial power of attorney

Date: 2026-06-13

Educational overview only; not legal advice.

## Purpose

A durable financial (property) power of attorney authorizes an agent (attorney-in-fact) to manage financial and legal matters if you are incapacitated or unavailable. Without it, family may need court guardianship or conservatorship — slow and costly.

## Typical agent powers

- Pay bills and taxes<br>- Manage bank and brokerage accounts<br>- Buy/sell or maintain real property<br>- Handle insurance and government benefits<br>- Operate a small business interest<br>- Communicate with financial institutions

Scope can be broad or limited; springing vs immediate effective date varies by state form and drafting.

## Illinois

Illinois Power of Attorney Act provides a statutory short form durable power of attorney for property (755 ILCS 45 Article III). Separate from the health care POA (Article IV). Both are typically part of a complete Illinois estate plan alongside RLT, pour-over will, and advance directives.

  </tbody>

During incapacity, unfunded assets are why POA remains essential even with a living trust. Goal: fund the trust and keep POA current as backup.

## Agent selection

Choose someone trustworthy, organized, and willing to act. Consider naming successors. Discuss expectations; agents owe fiduciary duties.

## Revocation and updates

Revoke prior POAs in writing when executing new forms. Notify institutions holding old copies. Update after major life events.
